Refraction of Light

Definition:

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one transparent medium to another, causing a change in its speed and direction.

Key Concepts:

  • Incident Ray: The incoming ray of light.
  • Refracted Ray: The ray of light that bends as it enters a new medium.
  • Normal: An imaginary line perpendicular to the boundary between the two media at the point of incidence.
  • Angle of Incidence: The angle between the incident ray and the normal.
  • Angle of Refraction: The angle between the refracted ray and the normal.

Snell's Law:

Snell's Law describes the relationship between the angles of incidence and refraction and the refractive indices of the two media involved: n1 * sin θ1 = n2 * sin θ2.

Factors Affecting Refraction:

  • Change in Speed: Refraction occurs when light travels from one medium to another with a different optical density, causing a change in speed.
  • Change in Direction: The change in speed leads to a change in the direction of light.
  • Refraction in Lenses: Convex and concave lenses refract light differently, leading to diverse optical effects.

Applications:

Refraction has various practical applications, including the functioning of lenses in eyeglasses, cameras, microscopes, and telescopes.

Understanding the principles of refraction is crucial in optics and helps explain phenomena like the bending of a straw in a glass of water or the formation of rainbows in the sky.
